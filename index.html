It's Dark and Hell Is Hot:

Did this daring debut revitalize east-coast gangster rap?

By Gabriella Bulgarelli
Sunday, April 23rd, 2018
This is my second installment in my data driven rap series. You can view the first project, a look at Kanye West's sampling history, here.

Mischevous Man

Ever since the release of his first album, Its Dark and Hell is Hot (1998), DMX has been making headlines. He has been in and out of prison and rehab, has created fashion lines and even broadcast his reunion with his son. In this data dashboard we will explore the discography of this highly prolific rapper, paying specific attention to his first release and how it changed the landscape of rap at the time.

It has to be said...

but I myself don't particularly like DMX or his music. I find myself offended by his lyrics, and in awe of his flippant, mercurial, and agressive behavior in the media. I've heard Kendrick talk about how DMX's song "Damien" influenced him. I've watched the video of him learning to use Google and it did not evoke the same level of compassion that my mother does when my siblings try to teach her, but I am trying to keep an open mind while working on this project, and I am hoping to learn something.

I even saw him post about Christmas music? Maybe this is not a song that needed to exist; but, although it is obvious that objectivity is almost entirely impossible moving forward, I hope it will not affect my presentation of this data. Let's dive in.

DMX's Upbringing

DMX was born Earl Simmons in December 1970 in Mount Vernon, New York. He was brought up as a Jehovah's Witness, and his childhood was riddled with abuse, arrests, and stints in group homes.

The Simplest Lexicon in the Game

It may not come as a surprise to some, but DMX has the worst vocabulary in the rap game. The survey, which was done by Matt Daniels at The Pudding in 2017 (shown below) ranks him the lowest of all 85 rappers included; but, just slightly lower than other East Coast wordsmiths 50 Cent, Nicki Minaj, and Salt N Pepa.

As seen above, the most eloquent east-coast artists are:
  1. Aesop Rock
  2. GZA (only solo albums)
  3. Kool Keith
  4. RZA (only solo albums)

Is this lack of vocabulary neccesarily a problem?

I don't know if I can really say, however I'd like to leave a quote from Jay-Z's Moment of Clarityas food for thought:

"I dumbed down for my audience to double my dollars
They criticized me for it, yet they all yell "holla"
If skills sold, truth be told,
I'd probably be lyrically Talib Kweli
Truthfully I wanna rhyme like Common sense
But I did 5 mil - I ain't been rhyming like Common since"

DMX Album Certifications and Billboard Peak Positions

Haters will say its photoshopped, but most of DMX's albums have debuted or peaked at #1 on the Billboard chart. This bar graph highlights the album certifications and peak positions on the Billboard chart for DMX.

ALBUM TITLES ARE ABBREVIATED! See chart at the bottom of this page!

As seen above, the most commercially successful DMX albums are:
  1. (1998) Its Dark and Hell is Hot (US Sales of 4,674,000)
  2. (1999) ...And Then There Was X (US Sales of 4,950,000)
  3. (1998) Flesh of my Flesh, Blood of my Blood (US Sales of 3,417,000)

And the least commercially successful albms are:
  1. (2006) Year of the Dog, Again (US Sales of only 125,000, first DMX album that did not debut at #1 on the Billboard chart)
  2. (2012) Undisputed (US Sales of only 17,000 copies, and debuted at #19 on the Billboard chart)

*Remember-the goal here is to have the highest (lowest on this graph) peak position and the highest number of weeks on the chart!*
